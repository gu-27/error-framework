The Framework

Three Sources of Error

Every flawed decision can be examined through three distinct lenses. Understanding which type of error dominated helps you know what to fix — and what to forgive.

Overview

Why Errors Are Worth Classifying

Not all mistakes are created equal. Some errors stem from the person making the decision — their knowledge, biases, or intentions. Others emerge from the systems used to evaluate that decision. Still others are products of forces entirely outside anyone's control. Without a framework, we tend to blame the nearest person. With one, we can act more precisely.

Actor Error

The Decision-Maker's Contribution

Actor errors arise from the individual at the center of the decision. This includes cognitive shortcuts, limited expertise, conflicting incentives, or deliberate choice. The actor is who we most naturally assign blame to — but only one type of error is fully theirs.

Evaluator Error

The System's Role in Judgment

Evaluator errors occur in how a decision is assessed — not in the decision itself. Flawed metrics, biased reviewers, inconsistent criteria, or misaligned incentive structures all produce evaluator error. The decision may have been reasonable; the evaluation was not.

Situational Error

Context Beyond Anyone's Control

Situational errors emerge from the environment surrounding a decision — timing, information availability, resource constraints, or unpredictable external events. Even ideal actors in well-designed systems can produce poor outcomes when the situation itself is hostile to good decisions.

Key Insight

Most Real Errors Are Mixed

In practice, bad outcomes reflect all three error types in varying proportions. The framework is most powerful not as a blame-assignment tool, but as a diagnostic one. By distributing responsibility across actor, evaluator, and situation, you surface what actually needs to change — training, systems redesign, or environmental support — rather than simply finding someone to hold accountable.
